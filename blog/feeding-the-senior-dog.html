Nutrition becomes critical as dogs age. Metabolism slows, digestion changes, and supplement marketing overwhelms. Navigate with evidence.

Changing Nutritional Needs

  • Decreased Metabolism: Fewer calories needed as activity decreases
  • Protein Needs: Healthy seniors need 50% MORE protein than young adults to prevent muscle loss (sarcopenia)
  • Digestive Changes: Reduced enzyme production affects absorption
  • Dental Issues: May require softer food
  • Disease Management: Chronic conditions require dietary modifications (see care team article)

"Senior" Food Reality: No AAFCO regulatory definition. Most are adult formulas with slightly less fat and more fiber. Individual needs vary enormously—assess your dog, not their age.

Weight Management: Most Important Intervention

Excess weight worsens arthritis pain, increases cardiovascular strain, triggers diabetes, reduces lifespan.

Body Condition Score (BCS):

  • Ideal (4-5/9): Ribs palpable with slight pressure, visible waist, abdominal tuck
  • Overweight (6-7/9): Ribs difficult to feel, minimal waist/tuck
  • Obese (8-9/9): Ribs not palpable, no waist, distended abdomen

Weight Loss Strategy:

  • Vet-determined calorie target
  • Measure food (no eyeballing)
  • Treats <10% daily calories
  • 1-2% body weight loss per week
  • Add low-calorie vegetables for satiety
  • Gentle exercise helps but insufficient alone

Protein: Don't Restrict

Unless Stage 3+ Kidney Disease, DO NOT restrict protein. Healthy seniors need ~50% more protein. Source: Tufts Petfoodology

Digestive Sensitivities

Common Issues: Food sensitivities, reduced motility, decreased enzymes, medication side effects

Management:

  • Highly digestible diets (prescription if needed)
  • Smaller, frequent meals (2-3x daily)
  • Gradual transitions (7-10 days)
  • Probiotics for gut flora
  • Limited ingredient diets if sensitivities
  • Adequate fiber, hydration

Special Dietary Considerations

  • Kidney Disease: Reduced quality protein, restricted phosphorus, prescription diets, increased moisture
  • Heart Disease: Reduced sodium, increased taurine/L-carnitine, omega-3s, cardiac diets
  • Diabetes: High fiber, complex carbs, consistent meal timing with insulin, weight management
  • Arthritis: Weight management primary; omega-3s and supplements (see below); pain management details

Supplements: Evidence vs Marketing

Nutritional perspective below. See pain management article for comprehensive pain protocols.

Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA):

  • Benefits: Anti-inflammatory for arthritis, kidney disease, heart disease, cognition
  • Dosing: 50-100 mg/kg combined EPA/DHA; arthritis = 100mg/kg; emphasize EPA for joints
  • Reality: 30kg dog needs ~3000mg EPA/DHA daily = 3-4 capsules, not one. Most under-dose.
  • Quality: Veterinary-grade. See VCA Fish Oil Guide

Glucosamine/Chondroitin:

  • Reality: Poor oral bioavailability (~10-12%). JAVMA Study
  • Better: Injectable Adequan (proven efficacy); oral Dasuquin has clinical evidence
  • Expectations: Mild benefit at best; not substitute for proven pain medications

MCT Oil: Cognitive function support; Purina NeuroCare/Bright Mind show improvement in 30 days

SAMe: Liver/cognitive support for liver disease or dysfunction

Limited Evidence: Antioxidants (better from food), CoQ10 (limited canine research)

Supplement Guidelines

  • Discuss with veterinarian
  • Quality matters—use reputable brands
  • "Natural" ≠ safe or effective
  • Watch medication interactions
  • Skeptical of marketing claims

Practical Feeding Challenges

Decreased Appetite:

  • Warm food for aroma
  • Low-sodium broth/palatable toppers
  • Hand-feeding or elevated bowls (see mobility aids)
  • Rule out dental pain, nausea
  • Appetite stimulants if appropriate

Unintended Weight Loss:

  • High-Calorie Recovery Diets: Prescription (Hill's a/d, Royal Canin Recovery) for maximum nutrition in small volumes
  • Highly palatable to encourage eating
  • Consult vet if losing weight despite adequate food
  • Track weight weekly

Difficulty Eating: Soften kibble or switch to canned; elevated bowls; non-slip mats; address dental disease

Hydration

Risk Factors: Reduced thirst drive, mobility issues, medications, chronic diseases

Checking Dehydration:

  • Skin Tent Test: Pinch neck skin—should snap back immediately (>1-2 sec = dehydrated)
  • Gum Test: Should be moist/slippery (sticky = dehydrated); capillary refill <2 sec. AKC Guide

Encouraging Hydration:

  • Multiple water stations
  • Fresh water frequently changed
  • Water fountains
  • Add water to food
  • Low-sodium broth, ice cubes as treats

When to Consult Vet

  • Unexplained weight changes
  • Persistent vomiting/diarrhea
  • Decreased appetite >1 day
  • Chronic disease diagnosis
  • Before significant diet changes or new supplements

Nutrition impacts comfort, energy, and quality of life. Individualize, stay observant, adjust as needed. Partner with veterinary team.

Action Steps

  • Assess BCS
  • Measure food properly
  • Review supplements with vet
  • Evaluate hydration, add stations
  • Food journal for digestive issues
