The Problem: This question comes up a lot, and while the responses are usually well-intentioned, it touches on several important issues. I ended up writing a full article to explain them rather than repeating the same explanation each time.

Level 1: You've Diagnosed a Symptom, Not a Problem

"stiffness after exercise" and "occasional limping" are clinical signs, not a diagnosis. They tell you nothing about:

  • Which structure is painful (joint? tendon? ligament? bone? nerve?)
  • What's causing the pain (osteoarthritis? soft tissue injury? bone cancer? tick-borne disease? autoimmune disorder?)
  • How severe the damage is
  • Whether rest, medication, surgery, or something else is appropriate

"Limping" in a senior dog can be anything from mild arthritis to osteosarcoma to a ruptured cruciate ligament to Lyme disease. These require radically different treatments. Some are time-sensitive.

üö® Red Flag: Limping Can Be Serious

Osteosarcoma (bone cancer) is common in large-breed senior dogs and often presents as gradual lameness. If you waste weeks on CBD while cancer spreads, you've lost precious time. A vet visit with radiographs can rule this out quickly.

Level 2: CBD Oil Is Not Pain Management

CBD oil may have a role in chronic pain management‚Äîas one piece of a multi-modal approach, under veterinary supervision. What it is not:

  • A primary analgesic (pain reliever)
  • A replacement for evidence-based pain medications like NSAIDs or gabapentinoids (see Multi-Modal Pain Management: Medications for what actually works)
  • A diagnostic tool
  • A cure for whatever is causing your dog's pain

The Cornell study everyone cites showed dogs with osteoarthritis had improved mobility scores when CBD was added to their existing treatment. Key words: added to. Not "instead of." Not "by itself." And critically: they had a diagnosis of osteoarthritis first.

If your dog has meaningful pain, they likely need actual medications‚ÄîNSAIDs, gabapentin or pregabalin, amantadine, or newer options like Librela. These require veterinary prescription and monitoring. (See Multi-Modal Pain Management: Medications for the full breakdown.)

Level 3: You Don't Know What You're Buying (And You're Probably Breaking the Law)

Here's the brutal truth: In Canada, there are zero Health Canada-approved CBD drugs for animals. This means every bottle of "Pet CBD" you see in a pet store, gas station, or unauthorized website is an illegal, unregulated grey-market product.

These products are not subject to federal testing or oversight. Studies have repeatedly found that unregulated CBD products often contain:

  • Less CBD than the label claims (sometimes zero)
  • More THC than the label claims (THC is toxic to dogs)
  • Contaminants (pesticides, heavy metals, solvents, mold)
  • Wildly inconsistent batch-to-batch dosing

When you buy "Pet CBD" from an unregulated source, you are trusting a random company to not poison your dog. There is no government oversight ensuring product safety or accuracy.

The Canadian Solution: Buy Human CBD from Licensed Retailers

If you're going to use CBD (under veterinary supervision, added to appropriate baseline pain medications), buy it from provincially licensed cannabis retailers like OCS (Ontario), BC Cannabis Stores, or equivalent provincial retailers.

Why human-grade cannabis products?

  • Regulated Supply Chain: Health Canada strictly regulates human cannabis products
  • Guaranteed Chemistry: If the label says 20mg/ml CBD, federal law ensures it actually contains 20mg/ml
  • Mandatory Safety Testing: All products must be tested for pesticides, mold, heavy metals, and residual solvents
  • Consistent Dosing: Each batch is verified, not guesswork

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: How to Buy Human CBD Safely for Your Dog

You cannot just buy "whatever" human CBD product. Many contain high THC or additives that are safe for humans but toxic to dogs. Follow these rules:

1. Buy CBD Isolate Only

  • Look specifically for CBD Isolate oils (not "full spectrum" or "broad spectrum")
  • Check the legal label‚Äîit should read:
    • THC: < 1 mg/g (or "ND" - Non-Detectable)
    • CBD: Your desired potency (usually 20-50 mg/g)

2. Life-or-Death Ingredient Check

  • NO Xylitol: This artificial sweetener causes rapid liver failure in dogs. Found in some human elixirs and edibles. FATAL.
  • NO Added Terpenes/Flavors: Buy "Unflavored" or "Natural" only. Human additives like peppermint oil or citrus terpenes can cause GI distress or toxicity
  • Carrier Oil: Most legal Canadian oils use MCT oil (coconut derivative), which is generally safe for dogs (may cause loose stool if you start too high)

3. Do the Math and Use Proper Measuring

  • Human products are concentrated (e.g., 30mg/ml or 50mg/ml)
  • Calculate carefully: If your dog needs 5mg and your oil is 50mg/ml, that's 0.1 ml
  • Buy a 1ml oral syringe from a pharmacy‚Äîbottle droppers are inaccurate
  • Never guess. Get the dose right.

Level 4: "What Dose Do I Give?" Is the Wrong Question

The fact that you're asking strangers for dosing advice for a product you haven't purchased for a condition you haven't diagnosed tells me everything about where this is headed.

Even if CBD were appropriate for your dog's (undiagnosed) condition:

  • Dosing depends on the concentration of the specific product
  • Dosing depends on your dog's weight
  • Dosing depends on what other medications your dog is on (CBD affects liver metabolism)
  • Dosing depends on whether your dog has liver or kidney disease
  • Effective dosing ranges from 1-5mg/kg twice daily‚Äîthat's a 5x range

The answer to "what dose" is: Whatever your veterinarian recommends after examining your dog and reviewing their bloodwork.

Level 5: You're Delaying Real Help

Here's the part that actually makes me angry.

Your dog is telling you something is wrong. They're sore. They're limping. That's your dog communicating the only way they can: "This hurts."

And the response is to crowdsource supplement recommendations from strangers instead of going to someone who can actually examine the dog, diagnose the problem, and provide effective treatment?

I understand veterinary care is expensive. I understand people feel overwhelmed. I understand the appeal of a "natural" option you can buy online without an appointment.

But your dog is in pain right now. Every day you spend waiting for Amazon deliveries and titrating up unregulated supplements is a day your dog spends uncomfortable‚Äîor a day a serious condition progresses.

What You Should Actually Do

  1. Make a vet appointment. Not eventually. Now. Describe exactly what you're seeing: "sore after walks, occasional limping, started [timeframe]."
  2. Get diagnostics. At minimum, a physical exam. Probably radiographs. Maybe bloodwork depending on what's found.
  3. Get a diagnosis. What structure is affected? What's the underlying cause?
  4. Discuss pain management options. This might include NSAIDs, gabapentin, adequan, Librela, physical therapy, weight management‚Äîand yes, possibly CBD as an adjunct.
  5. Monitor and adjust. Pain management is ongoing. Use the tools in Quality of Life Assessments to track whether treatment is working.

Can't Afford a Full Workup?

Be honest with your vet about budget constraints. Many will work with you to prioritize: "Given the budget, let's start with radiographs of that leg and a basic exam, and we'll go from there." Something is better than nothing, and nothing is what you get from Facebook recommendations.

The Role CBD Can Play

To be clear: I'm not saying CBD is useless. In Reiker's palliative protocol, CBD was part of his multi-modal regimen. It was added after he was on an NSAID, pregabalin, and acetaminophen‚Äîand it was added under veterinary guidance with monitoring.

CBD may help with:

  • Mild anxiety
  • Supplemental anti-inflammatory effects
  • Possibly some analgesic contribution (evidence is mixed)

CBD is a reasonable addition to a pain plan. It is not a pain plan.

Summary: What's Wrong with "Where Do I Buy CBD?"

  1. No diagnosis: You don't know what's causing the pain
  2. Wrong treatment: CBD alone doesn't effectively manage pain
  3. Unregulated products: You have no idea what you're actually giving
  4. No dosing guidance: Strangers can't dose your specific dog
  5. Delayed care: Your dog suffers while you experiment

Your dog deserves better than crowdsourced healthcare. Go to the vet.
