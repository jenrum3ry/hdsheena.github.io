Subtle Signs, Assessment Tools, and When "Slowing Down" is Actually Pain

Dogs hide pain instinctively. "They're just slowing down" is often chronic pain that should be treated.

Why Pain Recognition Matters

Unmanaged pain accelerates decline. Per 2022 AAHA Guidelines, early intervention is critical. Chronic pain causes:

  • Muscle atrophy from decreased activity
  • Behavioral changes affecting human-animal bond
  • Appetite and weight changes
  • Sleep disruption affecting healing
  • Elevated stress hormones worsening other conditions

Good news: Managing pain often produces dramatic improvement.

Subtle Signs of Pain

Dogs rarely vocalize chronic pain. Watch for subtle behavioral changes:

Mobility and Movement

  • Hesitation: Before stairs, jumping, furniture access
  • Stiffness: After rest; needs "warm-up" steps
  • Altered Gait: Bunny-hopping, limping, shortened stride, head bobbing
  • Difficulty Rising: Multiple attempts, using furniture for support
  • Reduced Activity: Less interest in walks, shorter play, more lying down
  • Weight Shifting: Frequent position changes, difficulty settling

Behavioral Changes

  • Social Withdrawal: Avoiding family activities, other pets
  • Greeting Changes: Not coming to door, staying in place
  • Touch Sensitivity: Moving away, tensing, avoiding previously favorite petting spots
  • Irritability: Snapping or growling when touched
  • Vocalization: Whining, groaning during movement, nighttime vocalization
  • Restlessness: Pacing, unable to settle

Physical Signs

  • Muscle Tension: Tense face, furrowed brow, tight back muscles
  • Panting: At rest without heat/exercise
  • Posture Changes: Hunched back, tucked abdomen ("prayer position" for abdominal pain), weight-shifting
  • Licking/Chewing: Focused on specific joints
  • Trembling: Unrelated to temperature

Routine Changes

  • Sleep: Difficulty settling, frequent waking, location changes
  • Eating: Reluctance to bend to bowls, eating slower, decreased interest
  • Grooming: Reduced self-care, excessive licking of joints
  • Elimination: Reluctance to posture, accidents from mobility issues

Critical Signs Requiring Immediate Attention

  • Sudden inability to stand or support weight
  • Crying out with movement or when touched
  • Extreme restlessness or inability to settle at all
  • Rapid breathing or panting at rest
  • Refusal to eat or drink for more than 24 hours
  • Significant behavior changes like aggression in previously gentle dogs

Pain Assessment Tools

Validated tools to quantify and track pain:

Canine Brief Pain Inventory (CBPI)

Owner-assessed tool for pain severity and daily function. UPenn CBPI Tool scores pain levels and functional interference.

Liverpool Osteoarthritis in Dogs (LOAD)

LOAD Questionnaire—gold standard for home arthritis tracking. Use for disease progression monitoring and treatment evaluation.

Helsinki Chronic Pain Index (HCPI)

HCPI focuses on behavioral pain signs during daily activities (rising, walking, jumping).

Glasgow Composite Measure Pain Scale

Veterinary setting tool evaluating posture, vocalization, mobility, touch response.

Video Documentation

  • Record walking, rising, stairs
  • Compare over time and post-treatment
  • Show to vet—captures behavior missed during office visits

Aging vs. Pain Distinction

Normal Aging:

  • Gradual changes
  • Willingness to participate at slower pace
  • Rest provides relief
  • Comfortable when resting
  • Stable personality

Pain Indicators:

  • Sudden or marked changes
  • Activity avoidance
  • Difficulty settling
  • Restlessness after rest
  • Personality changes (withdrawal, irritability)
  • Improvement with pain medication

The Diagnostic Trial Approach

When uncertain if changes are pain-related, try medication trial. WSAVA Guidelines recognize "Response to Therapy" as valid diagnostic. Improvement within days-weeks confirms pain involvement. Often more practical than extensive diagnostics.

Pain Tracking System

  • Daily Logs: Activity, appetite, sleep, mobility
  • Calendar Tracking: Mark good/bad days
  • Video Library: Regular mobility recordings
  • Monthly Assessments: Use standardized tools
  • Medication Notes: Timing and observed changes

Communicating with Your Vet

Bring to appointments:

  • Pain logs and videos
  • Specific behaviors ("difficulty rising after rest" not "seems uncomfortable")
  • What improves/worsens symptoms
  • Quality of life impacts

Recognizing pain means becoming attuned to your dog's communication and taking action now.

Action Steps

  • Start pain journal today
  • Take baseline mobility videos
  • Complete validated pain assessment
  • Review pain signs with family
  • Schedule vet appointment for concerns
