Pain Recognition in Senior Dogs

Subtle Signs, Assessment Tools, and When "Slowing Down" is Actually Pain

← Back to Blog Series

Dogs are masters at hiding pain. This evolutionary trait, designed to protect them from appearing vulnerable to predators, makes our job as caregivers significantly more challenging. The phrase "they're just slowing down" is one of the most common—and most dangerous—assumptions we make about senior dogs. Often, what looks like normal aging is actually chronic pain that can and should be treated.

Why Pain Recognition Matters

Unmanaged pain doesn't just reduce quality of life—it accelerates decline. Chronic pain leads to:

  • Decreased activity, which causes muscle atrophy and weakens joints further
  • Changes in behavior that can damage the human-animal bond
  • Reduced appetite and potential weight loss or gain
  • Sleep disruption, which affects healing and cognitive function
  • Increased stress hormones that can worsen other health conditions

The good news: when we identify and manage pain, we often see dramatic improvements that give us more quality time with our senior companions.

Subtle Signs of Pain in Dogs

Dogs rarely yelp or cry out due to chronic pain. Instead, they show us through behavior changes that are easy to overlook or misinterpret:

Mobility and Movement

  • Hesitation: Pausing before stairs, jumping into cars, or getting on furniture they previously accessed easily
  • Stiffness: Particularly after rest, taking several steps to "warm up"
  • Altered Gait: Bunny-hopping, limping (even subtle), shortened stride, head bobbing while walking
  • Difficulty Rising: Taking multiple attempts to stand, using furniture for support, or needing a "warm-up" stretch
  • Reduced Activity: Less interest in walks, shorter play sessions, spending more time lying down
  • Positional Changes: Shifting weight frequently while standing, difficulty finding a comfortable position to lie down

Behavioral Changes

  • Decreased Social Interaction: Withdrawing from family activities, avoiding other pets
  • Changes in Greeting Behavior: Not coming to the door, staying in place instead of getting up to greet
  • Altered Response to Petting: Moving away from touch, tensing up, or changes in favorite petting locations
  • Increased Irritability: Snapping or growling when touched, particularly around painful areas
  • Vocalization: Whining, groaning when lying down or getting up, or increased vocalization at night
  • Restlessness: Pacing, inability to settle, frequent position changes

Physical Signs

  • Muscle Tension: Tense facial expression, furrowed brow, tight muscles along the back
  • Panting: Without exercise or heat, particularly if accompanied by restlessness
  • Changes in Posture: Hunched back, tucked abdomen, weight-shifting to favor certain limbs
  • Licking or Chewing: Focused attention on specific joints or areas, even without visible wounds
  • Trembling: Not related to temperature, particularly in limbs or while standing

Changes in Routine

  • Sleep Pattern Changes: Difficulty settling, frequent waking, changes in preferred sleeping locations
  • Appetite Changes: Reluctance to bend to eat/drink, eating slower, decreased interest in food
  • Altered Grooming: Reduced self-grooming, difficulty reaching certain areas, or excessive licking of painful areas
  • Bathroom Issues: Reluctance to posture for elimination, accidents due to difficulty getting up in time

Critical Signs Requiring Immediate Attention

  • Sudden inability to stand or support weight
  • Crying out with movement or when touched
  • Extreme restlessness or inability to settle at all
  • Rapid breathing or panting at rest
  • Refusal to eat or drink for more than 24 hours
  • Significant behavior changes like aggression in previously gentle dogs

Pain Assessment Tools

Several validated tools can help quantify pain and track changes over time:

Canine Brief Pain Inventory (CBPI)

This owner-assessed tool evaluates pain severity and pain interference with daily activities. It scores:

  • Pain at its worst, least, average, and current level
  • How pain interferes with general activity, enjoyment of life, and ability to walk, run, climb, and rise

Helsinki Chronic Pain Index (HCPI)

Focuses on behavioral signs of chronic pain in everyday life situations:

  • Observes behavior during activities like rising, walking, trotting, and jumping
  • Assesses vocalization, mood, and changes in activity level

Glasgow Composite Measure Pain Scale

Primarily used in veterinary settings but useful for owner awareness:

  • Evaluates posture, vocalization, attention to painful area
  • Assesses mobility and response to touch

Video Documentation

Sometimes the best assessment tool is your smartphone:

  • Record your dog walking, rising from rest, navigating stairs
  • Capture behaviors you're concerned about
  • Compare videos over time or after starting treatment
  • Show videos to your veterinarian—they can see things that might be missed during an office visit when adrenaline is high

The "Slowing Down" vs. Pain Distinction

How do you differentiate normal aging from pain that needs treatment? Consider these factors:

Normal Aging Typically Shows:

  • Gradual, consistent changes over time
  • Willingness to participate in activities at a gentler pace
  • Good response to rest
  • Comfort when resting
  • Maintenance of social interaction and personality

Pain Typically Shows:

  • More sudden or noticeable changes
  • Avoidance of previously enjoyed activities
  • Difficulty settling or finding comfortable positions
  • Restlessness even after rest
  • Personality or behavioral changes (withdrawal, irritability)
  • Improvement with pain medication (diagnostic trial)

The Diagnostic Trial Approach

When you're uncertain whether changes are pain-related, your veterinarian may suggest a diagnostic trial with pain medication. If you see improvement in mobility, activity level, or attitude within days to a couple weeks, you have confirmation that pain was a factor. This approach is often more practical and less stressful than extensive diagnostics in senior dogs.

Creating a Pain Tracking System

Systematic tracking helps you identify patterns and measure treatment effectiveness:

  • Daily Logs: Note activity level, appetite, sleep quality, mobility issues
  • Good Day/Bad Day Tracking: Simple calendar marking with colors or symbols
  • Video Library: Regular videos showing mobility and activity
  • Monthly Pain Assessments: Complete a standardized tool monthly to track trends
  • Medication Response Notes: Record when medications are given and any changes observed

Communicating Pain to Your Veterinarian

When discussing pain with your veterinary team:

  • Bring your pain logs and videos
  • Describe specific behaviors, not just general impressions ("difficulty rising after rest" rather than "seems uncomfortable")
  • Note what makes things better or worse
  • Share how pain is affecting quality of life
  • Be honest about what you're observing, even if you're worried about the implications

Remember: recognizing pain isn't about feeling guilty for missing signs earlier. It's about becoming more attuned to your dog's communication and taking action to improve their comfort now.

Action Steps

  • Start a pain observation journal today
  • Take baseline videos of your dog's mobility and activities
  • Complete a pain assessment using one of the validated tools
  • Review the list of subtle pain signs with family members so everyone is watching
  • Schedule a veterinary appointment to discuss any concerns
