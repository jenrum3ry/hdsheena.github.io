The Ketamine Conversation

Understanding Ketamine Therapy for Chronic Pain and Informed Discussions with Vets

← Back to Blog Series

Mention "ketamine" to most people and they might think of anesthesia, emergency medicine, or its controversial reputation as a party drug. But in veterinary pain management, ketamine is emerging as a valuable tool for treating chronic pain that hasn't adequately responded to conventional therapies. For many senior dogs suffering despite multi-modal pain management, ketamine therapy can be transformative. Yet it remains underutilized, partly due to misconceptions and partly due to lack of awareness. Let's demystify this treatment option.

What is Ketamine?

Ketamine is a medication with multiple clinical uses:

  • Anesthetic Agent: Used at higher doses for general anesthesia or sedation
  • Analgesic: Provides pain relief through unique mechanisms
  • NMDA Receptor Antagonist: This is the key to its chronic pain benefits

For chronic pain management, we use much lower doses than those used for anesthesia—subanesthetic doses that provide pain relief without significant sedation or dissociation.

How Ketamine Works for Chronic Pain

Ketamine's pain-relieving properties work differently from conventional pain medications:

NMDA Receptor Antagonism

Chronic pain involves a process called "central sensitization" or "wind-up"—a clinical term owners should know when discussing pain management with their vet. Wind-up occurs when the nervous system becomes increasingly sensitive to pain signals over time. NMDA receptors play a key role in this amplification. Ketamine blocks these receptors, essentially "turning down" the volume on pain signals that have been amplified. Learn more about Multi-Modal Pain Management at VCA Hospitals.

Multiple Mechanisms

Ketamine also:

  • Reduces inflammation
  • Modulates opioid receptors
  • Affects serotonin and dopamine pathways
  • May have neuroprotective effects

This multi-modal action makes it particularly effective for chronic pain that involves both inflammatory and neuropathic components—common in senior dogs with severe arthritis or degenerative conditions.

Ketamine Administration for Chronic Pain

Constant Rate Infusion (CRI)

The most common approach:

  • Setting: Administered in a veterinary hospital setting
  • Duration: Typically 4-6 hours, sometimes longer
  • Monitoring: Continuous monitoring during infusion
  • Frequency: May be repeated every few weeks to months based on response

Oral Ketamine

Important Reality Check on Oral Ketamine

Oral ketamine has poor bioavailability in dogs (approximately 17%) and is highly variable due to first-pass liver metabolism. Plasma concentrations may be undetectable in some dogs after oral administration. While it is an option, oral ketamine is significantly less effective than injectable forms. Do not judge ketamine's potential for your dog based on oral trials alone—if oral ketamine fails, injectable routes may still provide benefit.

  • Compounded: Prepared by compounding pharmacies
  • Daily or Multiple Times Daily: Lower doses given regularly
  • Convenience: Can be given at home
  • Limitations: Bioavailability is poor compared to injectable routes; many dogs do not respond; should be considered the least effective administration route

Other Routes

  • Subcutaneous (SQ) Injections: An increasingly popular option for at-home chronic pain management
    • Typical Dosing: 0.5mg/kg given subcutaneously
    • Loading Phase: Many protocols require a "loading period" with more frequent injections (e.g., every 1-3 days for the first week) before seeing full benefits. The duration of effect for a single SQ injection is short (hours), but the benefit for chronic pain comes from "resetting" the NMDA receptors, which can provide relief lasting days to weeks.
    • Maintenance Frequency: After loading, can range from biweekly to weekly; frequency may need to be increased if declining function is noticed between doses
    • Benefits: Can be administered at home once trained; less time-intensive and expensive than CRI; maintains consistent pain control; outpatient rather than hospital-based
    • Monitoring: Track functional response and duration of benefit to optimize dosing schedule
    • Considerations: Requires owner comfort with injections; needs close veterinary supervision; efficacy varies by individual
  • Transdermal: Limited evidence but being explored

Who Benefits from Ketamine Therapy?

Ideal Candidates

  • Senior dogs with chronic pain not adequately controlled by conventional medications
  • Dogs who can't tolerate NSAIDs or have contraindications to standard pain medications
  • Severe osteoarthritis causing significant functional impairment
  • Neuropathic pain conditions
  • Pain with suspected central sensitization (chronic pain that seems out of proportion to pathology)

Realistic Expectations

  • Not a Cure: Doesn't reverse arthritis or other underlying conditions
  • Variable Response: Some dogs have dramatic improvement; others have modest benefits or no response
  • Duration: Benefits may last weeks to months; repeated treatments often needed
  • Best as Part of Multi-Modal Plan: Most effective when combined with other pain management strategies

Success Stories

Many owners report their dogs regain function they thought was lost forever—playing again, showing more interest in activities, improved mobility, and better quality of life. Some dogs who were candidates for euthanasia due to poor pain control have gained months to years of good quality time after ketamine therapy.

Real-World Example: Some dogs begin with biweekly ketamine injections at 0.5mg/kg and maintain good function on that schedule. However, if you notice declining function between doses—increased difficulty standing, reduced activity, or other signs of breakthrough pain—the frequency can be increased to weekly injections to maintain consistent comfort and function.

Safety and Side Effects

During Infusion

At subanesthetic doses, side effects are generally minimal but may include:

  • Mild sedation
  • Nystagmus (rhythmic eye movements)
  • Increased salivation
  • Rarely, dysphoria or hallucinations (difficult to assess in dogs but generally brief and mild at these doses)

Post-Infusion

  • Most dogs return to normal quickly after infusion ends
  • Some have mild sedation for a few hours
  • Serious side effects are rare when properly dosed and monitored

With Oral Ketamine

  • Generally well-tolerated
  • Some dogs show mild behavioral changes
  • Requires careful dose finding for each individual

Contraindications

Ketamine should be used cautiously or avoided in dogs with:

  • Significant cardiac disease (can increase heart rate and blood pressure)
  • Uncontrolled hypertension
  • Seizure disorders (conflicting data; discuss with your vet)
  • Severe liver disease

Important Safety Note

Ketamine therapy should only be administered by or under the supervision of a veterinarian experienced with its use for chronic pain. The dosing for pain management is very different from anesthetic dosing, and proper monitoring is essential.

The Cost Factor

Ketamine therapy can be expensive:

  • CRI Infusion: Costs vary widely by location but typically $200-$600+ per session
  • Oral Ketamine: Generally less expensive per dose but ongoing cost
  • Repeated Treatments: Most dogs need multiple treatments over time

However, for dogs who respond well, many owners feel the quality of life improvement justifies the expense, especially compared to the cost of euthanasia for uncontrolled pain.

Having the Ketamine Conversation with Your Veterinarian

If Your Veterinarian is Familiar with Ketamine for Pain

Great! Discuss:

  • Whether your dog is a good candidate
  • Expected benefits and realistic outcomes
  • Protocol they use (dose, duration, monitoring)
  • Cost and frequency of treatments
  • What to expect during and after treatment

If Your Veterinarian is Unfamiliar or Hesitant

This is common—ketamine for chronic pain is still relatively new in some areas:

  • Share Information: Bring research articles or reviews on ketamine for chronic pain in dogs
  • Ask About Referral: Request referral to a pain management specialist or veterinary anesthesiologist
  • Don't Be Discouraged: If your vet isn't comfortable, finding one who is doesn't mean you're being difficult—you're being a good advocate
  • Telemedicine: Some pain specialists offer remote consultations to work with your local vet

Questions to Ask

  • Do you have experience using ketamine for chronic pain management?
  • What protocol do you recommend for my dog?
  • What monitoring will be done during treatment?
  • What response should I expect and how soon?
  • How do we measure success?
  • What are the alternatives if this doesn't work?
  • Are there veterinary pain specialists you can refer me to?

Script: What to Say to Your Vet

If you're not sure how to start the conversation, try this approach:

"I am concerned my dog may have central sensitization—the pain seems out of proportion to what we can see on imaging. I would like to discuss trying an NMDA antagonist. Would you be comfortable with a ketamine trial? If not, could we discuss Amantadine as a daily oral option, or can you refer me to a pain management specialist?"

Using the clinical term "central sensitization" signals that you've done your research and understand the mechanism being targeted.

Combining Ketamine with Other Therapies

Ketamine works best as part of a comprehensive pain management plan:

  • Continue Other Medications: NSAIDs, gabapentin, etc. unless specifically contraindicated
  • Physical Therapy: The improved comfort from ketamine may allow for more effective rehabilitation
  • Weight Management: Still crucial
  • Mobility Aids: Continue using supportive equipment
  • Alternative Therapies: Acupuncture, laser therapy, etc. can complement ketamine

Ketamine + Librela (Bedinvetmab): The Synergy Question

Owners often ask: "I'm already using Librela—do I still need ketamine?"

The answer is often YES. These medications work through completely different mechanisms and can be highly synergistic:

  • Librela is a monoclonal antibody that targets Nerve Growth Factor (NGF)—it addresses inflammation and peripheral nerve sensitization
  • Ketamine targets NMDA receptors in the spinal cord—it addresses central sensitization (wind-up pain)

For dogs with severe, long-standing arthritis, both peripheral and central pain pathways are often involved. Using both Librela and ketamine can provide more complete pain relief than either alone.

Amantadine: The "Daily Pill" Alternative

If ketamine injections aren't feasible for your situation, ask your veterinarian about Amantadine—an oral NMDA antagonist sometimes called "keto-lite." While not as potent as ketamine, it offers several advantages:

  • Oral administration: Given as a daily pill, no injections required
  • Similar mechanism: Also blocks NMDA receptors to reduce central sensitization
  • Well-tolerated: Generally has fewer side effects than many pain medications
  • Combination therapy: Often added to NSAIDs and gabapentin when standard protocols aren't providing enough relief

Amantadine can be a good stepping stone before committing to ketamine therapy, or a maintenance option for dogs who respond well to NMDA antagonism.

Monitoring Response

Track your dog's response systematically:

  • Use pain scoring tools before and after treatment
  • Take videos of mobility before and after to compare
  • Note changes in activity level, appetite, interaction
  • Track how long benefits last
  • Communicate findings to your veterinary team

When Ketamine May Not Be Enough

Even with optimized ketamine therapy, some dogs continue to struggle with pain or functional decline. In these challenging cases:

  • Diagnostic Trials: Consider trials with other strong analgesics (such as subcutaneous methadone given every 8 hours) as a final attempt to determine whether pain is the primary issue affecting function versus progressive neurological decline that won't respond to pain management alone.
    • Important Note: Methadone is a DEA-scheduled controlled substance requiring special prescribing authorization. Your veterinarian will need appropriate licensing and will discuss safety protocols.
  • Pain vs. Neurological Decline: The distinction between uncontrolled pain and neurological deterioration can become increasingly difficult to determine in advanced degenerative conditions; systematic trials can help clarify this
  • Pain Specialist Consultation: A board-certified veterinary pain management specialist or anesthesiologist may have additional options or insights
  • Palliative Care Focus: When pain is optimized but function continues to decline, it may be time to shift goals from attempting to restore function to focusing on comfort and quality of life
  • Quality of Life Re-evaluation: Use validated assessment tools regularly to determine if interventions are truly improving quality of life versus simply extending life

The Bottom Line on Ketamine

Ketamine isn't a magic bullet, and it's not appropriate for every dog. But for senior dogs suffering from chronic pain that hasn't adequately responded to conventional approaches, it represents a valuable option that can significantly improve quality of life. As awareness grows and more veterinarians gain experience with its use, ketamine is becoming an increasingly important tool in the palliative care toolkit.

If your senior dog is still uncomfortable despite optimal use of standard pain medications, don't accept "we've done all we can." Ask about ketamine. Your dog deserves every tool we have to offer them comfort in their golden years.

Action Steps

  • Research whether your current veterinary clinic offers ketamine therapy
  • Identify pain management specialists or veterinary anesthesiologists in your area
  • Document your dog's current pain level and function as a baseline
  • Prepare questions for your veterinarian about ketamine as an option
  • Join online communities where other owners share experiences with ketamine therapy
