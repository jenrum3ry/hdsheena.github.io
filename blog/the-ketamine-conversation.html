Ketamine is emerging as valuable for chronic pain unresponsive to conventional therapies. Underutilized due to misconceptions. Evidence supports subanesthetic dosing for chronic pain management.

What is Ketamine

Multiple clinical uses:

  • Anesthetic: Higher doses for sedation
  • Analgesic: Pain relief via unique mechanisms
  • NMDA Antagonist: Key for chronic pain

For chronic pain: subanesthetic doses provide relief without significant sedation.

How It Works

NMDA Receptor Antagonism: Blocks "wind-up" (central sensitization)—when nervous system amplifies pain signals over time. Ketamine turns down amplified pain volume. See VCA Multi-Modal Pain Management.

Multiple Mechanisms: Also reduces inflammation, modulates opioid receptors, affects serotonin/dopamine, may be neuroprotective.

Effective for chronic pain with inflammatory and neuropathic components—common in senior dogs with severe arthritis or degenerative conditions.

Administration Routes

Constant Rate Infusion (CRI):

  • Hospital setting, 4-6+ hours
  • Continuous monitoring required
  • Repeat every few weeks to months based on response

Oral Ketamine Reality

Poor bioavailability in dogs (~17%) with high variability. Plasma concentrations may be undetectable. Significantly less effective than injectable. Don't judge ketamine's potential based on oral trials—if oral fails, injectable may still help.

Oral (Compounded): Daily/multiple times daily; home convenience but poor bioavailability; least effective route

Subcutaneous (SQ) Injections: Increasingly popular for at-home chronic pain:

  • Dosing: 0.5mg/kg SQ
  • Loading Phase: More frequent (every 1-3 days for first week) before full benefits. Single injection effect is short (hours), but "resets" NMDA receptors for days-weeks relief
  • Maintenance: Biweekly to weekly; increase frequency if function declines between doses
  • Benefits: Home administration, less time/cost than CRI, consistent control, outpatient
  • Requires: Owner comfort with injections, close vet supervision; efficacy varies

Who Benefits

Ideal Candidates:

  • Chronic pain inadequately controlled by conventional medications
  • Can't tolerate NSAIDs or have contraindications
  • Severe osteoarthritis with functional impairment
  • Neuropathic pain
  • Suspected central sensitization (pain disproportionate to pathology)

Realistic Expectations:

  • Not a cure—doesn't reverse arthritis
  • Variable response (dramatic to modest to none)
  • Duration: weeks to months; repeated treatments often needed
  • Best as part of multi-modal plan

Success

Many dogs regain lost function—playing, improved mobility, better quality of life. Some euthanasia candidates gain months to years. Example: Biweekly 0.5mg/kg injections maintain function; if declining between doses, increase to weekly for consistent comfort.

Safety and Side Effects

During/Post-Infusion: Mild sedation, nystagmus, increased salivation; rarely dysphoria; most return to normal quickly

Oral: Generally well-tolerated, mild behavioral changes, requires dose finding

Contraindications: Significant cardiac disease, uncontrolled hypertension, seizure disorders (discuss), severe liver disease

Important

Administer only by/under supervision of experienced veterinarian. Dosing for pain ≠ anesthetic dosing. Proper monitoring essential.

Cost

  • CRI: $200-$600+ per session
  • Oral: Less expensive per dose but ongoing
  • Repeated Treatments: Most need multiple over time

For responding dogs, quality of life improvement often justifies expense vs euthanasia for uncontrolled pain.

Talking to Your Vet

If Vet Familiar: Discuss candidacy, expected outcomes, protocol, cost/frequency, what to expect

If Unfamiliar/Hesitant:

  • Share research articles
  • Request referral to pain specialist/anesthesiologist
  • Telemedicine consultations available
  • Being an advocate isn't difficult—it's necessary

Questions:

  • Experience with ketamine for chronic pain?
  • Recommended protocol?
  • Monitoring during treatment?
  • Expected response timeline?
  • Measuring success?
  • Alternatives if ineffective?
  • Specialist referrals?

Script

"I'm concerned my dog has central sensitization—pain seems disproportionate to imaging. I'd like to discuss an NMDA antagonist. Would you be comfortable with ketamine trial? If not, could we discuss Amantadine or refer to pain specialist?"

Using "central sensitization" signals you understand the targeted mechanism.

Combining with Other Therapies

Ketamine works best in comprehensive plan (see multi-modal pain article):

  • Continue NSAIDs, gabapentin unless contraindicated
  • Physical therapy (improved comfort enables effective rehab)
  • Weight management crucial (see feeding article)
  • Mobility aids (continue supportive equipment)
  • Alternative therapies (acupuncture, laser)

Ketamine + Librela Synergy: Often YES, still need both. Different mechanisms:

  • Librela: Targets NGF—addresses inflammation/peripheral sensitization
  • Ketamine: Targets NMDA—addresses central sensitization (wind-up)
  • Severe, long-standing arthritis involves both pathways; combination provides more complete relief

Amantadine: "Keto-Lite": Oral NMDA antagonist alternative if injections unfeasible:

  • Daily pill, no injections
  • Similar mechanism, less potent
  • Well-tolerated
  • Often added to NSAIDs+gabapentin when insufficient
  • Good stepping stone or maintenance option

Monitoring Response

  • Pain scoring tools before/after
  • Video mobility comparisons
  • Track activity, appetite, interaction changes
  • Track benefit duration
  • Communicate with vet team

When Ketamine Insufficient

  • Diagnostic Trials: Consider trials with strong analgesics (e.g., SQ methadone q8h) to determine if pain vs neurological decline (methadone = DEA controlled substance, special prescribing)
  • Pain vs Neuro: Distinction increasingly difficult in advanced degenerative conditions; trials clarify
  • Specialist: Board-certified pain specialist may have additional options
  • Palliative Focus: When pain optimized but function declines, shift to comfort and quality of life
  • QOL Re-evaluation: Use validated tools to determine if interventions truly improve quality vs simply extend life

Bottom Line

Not magic bullet, not for every dog. But for chronic pain unresponsive to conventional approaches, valuable option significantly improving quality of life. Awareness growing, more vets gaining experience.

If dog still uncomfortable despite optimal standard medications, don't accept "done all we can." Ask about ketamine. Your dog deserves every tool for comfort in golden years.

Action Steps

  • Research if current clinic offers ketamine
  • Identify pain specialists/anesthesiologists in area
  • Document current pain level and function as baseline
  • Prepare vet questions about ketamine
  • Join communities where owners share ketamine experiences
