Multi-Modal Pain Management

Different Medication Classes, Alternative Therapies, and Finding the Right Combination

← Back to Blog Series

"Multi-modal" pain management means attacking pain from multiple angles using different mechanisms of action. Rather than relying on a single medication at high doses—which may not fully control pain and increases side effect risks—we combine several approaches at lower, safer doses to achieve better overall pain control. Think of it as assembling a team where each player has a different role.

Why Multi-Modal Matters

Pain is complex, involving multiple pathways in the nervous system. Different types of pain—inflammatory, neuropathic, mechanical—respond to different treatments. By using multiple modalities:

  • We target different pain pathways simultaneously
  • We can often use lower doses of each medication, reducing side effects
  • We achieve better overall pain control than with single agents alone
  • We have backup options if one approach loses effectiveness or isn't tolerated

Pharmaceutical Options

Treatment Hierarchy: What Goes Where

First-Line Analgesics (can be used alone):

  • NSAIDs (if tolerated)
  • Librela (monoclonal antibody therapy)

Adjunct Therapies (should be added TO first-line treatment, not used alone for osteoarthritis pain):

  • Gabapentin or Pregabalin
  • Amantadine
  • CBD oil
  • Acetaminophen

Disease-Modifying Agents (can be used alone or with analgesics):

  • Adequan

Exception: For neuropathic pain (e.g., nerve injury, spinal disease), gabapentin or pregabalin may be appropriate first-line treatments.

First-Line Analgesics

Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs)

The gold-standard first-line treatment for inflammatory pain, particularly osteoarthritis:

  • Common Options: Carprofen (Rimadyl), meloxicam (Metacam), deracoxib (Deramaxx), firocoxib (Previcox), grapiprant (Galliprant)
  • How They Work: Reduce inflammation and pain at the source
  • Considerations: Require monitoring (bloodwork) as they can affect kidneys, liver, and GI tract; not all dogs can tolerate them long-term; grapiprant (a newer piprant class drug) may have fewer GI side effects
  • Not for: Dogs with kidney disease, liver disease, or GI ulcers without careful monitoring
  • Clinical Role: Can be used as sole analgesic for inflammatory pain; provides baseline pain control to which adjuncts can be added

Librela (Bedinvetmab)

A monoclonal antibody therapy that can serve as first-line treatment:

  • How It Works: Targets nerve growth factor, which is elevated in osteoarthritis pain
  • Administration: Monthly injection given in the veterinary clinic
  • Benefits: Very effective for many dogs; doesn't require daily medication; no systemic side effects on organs
  • Clinical Role: Adjunct to first-line analgesics; can be first-line for neuropathic pain (nerve injury, spinal disease) but should not be used alone for osteoarthritis
  • Best for: Adding to NSAIDs/Librela when baseline pain control is insufficient; primary treatment for neuropathic pain conditions

Amantadine

Originally an antiviral, now used as an NMDA receptor antagonist adjunct:

  • How It Works: Blocks "wind-up" phenomenon where chronic pain becomes amplified over time
  • Benefits: Once-daily dosing, generally well-tolerated, works synergistically with NSAIDs
  • Clinical Role: Adjunct only—should be added to NSAID or Librela, not used alone
  • Best for: Chronic pain that hasn't fully responded to first-line analgesics alone

Acetaminophen (Tylenol)

Can be used as an adjunct under veterinary supervision:

  • Dosing: Approximately 10mg/kg three times daily in dogs
  • Clinical Role: Adjunct to first-line analgesics; provides additional pain relief but insufficient alone for osteoarthritis
  • Critical Safety: Must never be given to cats; requires careful dosing to avoid liver toxicity in dogs
  • Monitoring: Watch for Dry Eye (KCS) with long-term use

Adjunct Analgesic Therapies

Important: The following medications are adjuncts—they should be added to baseline analgesic therapy (NSAID or Librela), not used alone for osteoarthritis pain. While they provide additional pain relief, they do not adequately address inflammatory pain on their own.

Gabapentin and Pregabalin

Originally anti-seizure medications, now widely used as adjuncts for chronic pain:

  • How They Work: Modulate nerve pain signals, particularly effective for neuropathic pain
  • Clinical Role: Typically used as adjunct or for breakthrough pain; may be appropriate as sole treatment in acute injury scenarios under veterinary guidance

Disease-Modifying Therapies

Adequan (Polysulfated Glycosaminoglycan)

  • Important: The 2022 AAHA Pain Management Guidelines no longer recommend tramadol as a first-line treatment for osteoarthritis due to lack of proven efficacy in dogs. It may still be used for its mild sedative properties or as a synergistic adjunct, but should not be relied upon as a primary analgesic.

    • Methadone:

    • How It Works: Full opioid agonist with NMDA receptor antagonist properties
    • Administration: Can be given subcutaneously every 8 hours for chronic pain management
    • Benefits: Dual mechanism (opioid + NMDA antagonism) may be particularly helpful for complex pain; can help determine if pain versus neurological dysfunction is the primary issue affecting function
    • Considerations: Requires frequent dosing; controlled substance requiring special prescribing; potential for sedation; useful as a diagnostic trial in severe cases

    Amantadine

    Originally an linical Role: Can be used alone or combined with analgesics; does not replace need for pain control in symptomatic dogs

  • Considerations: Requires injections (can be done at home after training); can be expensive

  • Administration: Initial series of injections (typically 8 over 4 weeks), then maintenance injections monthly or as needed
  • Benefits: Can slow disease progression, not just mask pain; generally very safe

    • Evidence-Based Medication Combination Examples

      Key Principle: Always start with first-line analgesic (NSAID or Librela), then add adjuncts as needed.

      • Mild-Moderate Osteoarthritis: NSAID alone or NSAID + Adequan injections (disease modification)
      • Moderate Pain Requiring Additional Control: NSAID + Gabapentin or Pregabalin (adjunct)
      • Severe Chronic Pain: NSAID + Gabapentin + Amantadine (multiple adjuncts to first-line therapy)
      • Cannot Tolerate NSAIDs: Librela (first-line alternative) + Gabapentin (adjunct if needed) + Adequan (disease modification)
      • Neuropathic Pain Primary: Gabapentin or Pregabalin (first-line for nerve pain) ± NSAID if concurrent inflammation
      • Breakthrough Pain: Baseline regimen + short-term opioids as directed by veterinarian
      • Advanced Multi-Modal Protocol (Stepwise Progression Example):
        • Step 1: NSAID (meloxicam 0.1mg/kg daily) — first-line baseline
        • Step 2: Added Pregabalin (2-4mg/kg BID) — adjunct when NSAID alone insufficient
        • Step 3: Added Acetaminophen (10mg/kg TID) — additional adjunct
        • Step 4: Added CBD oil (1-2mg/kg BID, titrating up) — complementary adjunct
        • Step 5: Progressed to weekly ketamine injections when all conventional approaches combined became insufficient

      What NOT to Do: Do not use gabapentin, amantadine, CBD, or acetaminophen alone for osteoarthritis without first-line analgesic coverage.

    Alternative and Complementary Therapies

    Laser Therapy (Class IV Therapeutic Laser)

    • How It Works: Photobiomodulation reduces inflammation, promotes healing, provides pain relief
    • Treatment: Regular sessions at veterinary clinic or rehabilitation center
    • Benefits: Non-invasive, no side effects, can target specific painful areas
    • Best for: Localized joint pain, wound healing, post-surgical recovery

    Acupuncture

    • How It Works: Stimulates specific points to release endorphins, improve blood flow, modulate pain signals
    • Evidence: Good evidence for effectiveness in chronic pain, particularly musculoskeletal pain
    • Treatment: Regular sessions with certified veterinary acupuncturist
    • Benefits: Minimal to no side effects, many dogs find it relaxing

    Physical Rehabilitation

    • Approaches: Therapeutic exercises, hydrotherapy, manual therapy, modalities like ultrasound or TENS
    • Benefits: Maintains muscle mass and joint mobility, provides controlled exercise, can be adapted as abilities change
    • Best for: Arthritis, post-surgical recovery, maintaining function in degenerative conditions

    Weight Management

    Technically not a therapy, but one of the most impactful interventions:

    • Every pound of excess weight adds significantly to joint stress
    • Studies show weight loss alone can dramatically improve arthritis symptoms
    • Often overlooked but should be part of every pain management plancomplementary support, not primary analgesic; use high-quality fish oil at appropriate doses (see Article 6: Feeding the Senior Dog for detailed dosing and quality guidance)
    • Glucosamine/Chondroitin: May support joint health; evidence is mixed but generally safe; not a pain medication (see Article 6 for more information)
    • Green-Lipped Mussel: Contains omega-3s and other compounds; some evidence for benefit; complementary support only
    • CBD Oil: Growing interest with clinical evidence from Cornell University showing benefit when added to existing treatment; adjunct only—should not be used alone for osteoarthritis pain; start at 1-2mg/kg twice daily and titrate up as needed (doses up to 5mg/kg are generally safe but higher doses often cause liver enzyme elevation); ALP elevation on bloodwork is a common, usually benign side effect—don't panic, but your vet should know; look for third-party tested products with certificates of analysis; legal status varies by location; may help with pain, anxiety, and inflammation when combined with appropriate baseline analgesics

      Important Notes on Supplements

      • Not all supplements are created equal—quality matters
      • "Natural" doesn't always mean safe or free from interactions
      • Discuss all supplements with your veterinarian
      • Supplements work best as part of comprehensive pain management, not as sole therapy

      Building Your Dog's Pain Plan

      The best pain management plan is individualized and flexible. For comprehensive, evidence-based guidance, the NC State Comparative Pain Research Center is an excellent authority on integrated pain management approaches.

      1. Start with a Foundation: Usually an NSAID (if tolerated) or alternative like Librela
      2. Add Complementary Medications: Based on pain type and severity (gabapentin or pregabalin for neuropathic pain, amantadine for chronic pain)
      3. Include Disease-Modifying Therapies: Like Adequan if appropriate
      4. Incorporate Non-Pharmaceutical Approaches: Weight management, physical therapy, laser, acupuncture
      5. Supplement Thoughtfully: Add quality supplements that make sense for your dog
      6. Reassess Regularly: What works today may need adjustment next month
      7. Follow a Logical Progression: Consider a stepwise approach—for example, starting with an NSAID, adding pregabalin when the NSAID alone becomes insufficient, then adding ketamine therapy when those combined approaches no longer provide adequate relief

      Monitoring and Adjusting

      Effective pain management requires ongoing assessment:

      • Track Response: Use pain scales and quality of life assessments
      • Watch for Side Effects: Know what to monitor for each medication
      • Establish First-Line Analgesic Coverage: Start with NSAID (if tolerated) or Librela—this is your foundation, not optional
      • Add Adjunct Medications as Needed: When first-line therapy alone is insufficient, add gabapentin/pregabalin, amantadine, or other adjuncts to the baseline analgesic (don't replace it)
      • Consider Disease-Modifying Therapies: Add Adequan to slow progression while maintaining analgesic coverage
      • Incorporate Non-Pharmaceutical Approaches: Weight management, physical therapy, laser, acupuncture enhance but don't replace pharmaceutical pain control
      • Use Supplements as Complementary Support: CBD, omega-3s, etc. are adjuncts to—not substitutes for—baseline analgesics
      • Reassess and Adjust Regularly: Pain management needs change; be prepared to add adjuncts sequentially as disease progresses
      • Follow Evidence-Based Progression: Example stepwise approach: NSAID → add pregabalin when insufficient → add amantadine → add acetaminophen → consider ketamine when conventional multi-modal approach no longer adequate

        • Critical: The Foundation Matters

        Attempting to manage osteoarthritis pain with only adjuncts (gabapentin, CBD, supplements) without first-line analgesic coverage is inadequate. If NSAIDs cannot be tolerated, use Librela as an alternative first-line agent, but establish baseline analgesic coverage before adding adjuncts.

        If conventional multi-modal approaches aren't providing adequate relief, don't give up. Options include:

        • Referral to a veterinary pain management specialist
        • Consideration of emerging therapies like ketamine (covered in detail in the next article)
        • Combination with interventional techniques (joint injections, nerve blocks)
        • Palliative care consultation to optimize overall comfort

        Pain management is both an art and a science. What works perfectly for one dog may not work for another. The goal is finding the combination that gives your dog the best quality of life with the fewest side effects—and being willing to adjust that combination as needs change.

        Action Steps

        • Review your dog's current pain management plan with your veterinarian
        • Discuss adding complementary approaches if relying on single medication
        • Research alternative therapies available in your area
        • Create a medication and treatment tracking system
        • Schedule regular reassessments to optimize the plan
