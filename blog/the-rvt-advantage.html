The RVT Advantage

Using Your Professional Knowledge to Advocate Effectively During Appointments

← Back to Blog Series

Being a Registered Veterinary Technician (RVT) or other veterinary professional with your own senior dog is a unique and sometimes difficult position. You have the knowledge to recognize problems early, understand treatment options, and spot warning signs that others might miss. Yet you also face challenges that lay pet owners don't encounter. You're simultaneously the trained professional and the emotional pet parent. Let's explore how to leverage your knowledge while navigating this dual role.

The Double-Edged Sword of Knowledge

The Advantages

  • Early Recognition: You spot subtle changes that others miss
  • Informed Questions: You know what to ask and what information matters
  • Technical Understanding: You comprehend diagnoses, treatment rationales, and medication mechanisms
  • Skill Set: You can perform many at-home care tasks (SQ fluids, wound care, medication administration)
  • Professional Network: Access to colleagues for advice and referrals
  • Realistic Expectations: Understanding of what's achievable and what's not

The Challenges

  • Hypervigilance: Seeing every subtle sign can lead to anxiety
  • Second-Guessing: Constantly questioning if you're doing enough
  • Emotional Distance Impossible: Can't compartmentalize like you do at work
  • Knowledge of Worst-Case Scenarios: You've seen how bad things can get
  • Anticipatory Grief: Starts earlier because you know what's coming
  • Professional-Personal Boundary Blur: Difficulty separating your roles

The Compassion Fatigue Factor

RVTs and other veterinary professionals often enter the profession because of their deep compassion for animals. When your own pet is suffering, it compounds the emotional toll of your daily work. Recognize this and seek support when needed.

Effective Advocacy During Appointments

Setting the Right Tone

How you introduce your professional status matters:

Helpful: "I'm an RVT, so I'm familiar with medical terminology and procedures, which might make our conversation easier."

Less Helpful: "I'm an RVT, so I already know what needs to be done."

The first approach invites collaboration; the second can create defensiveness.

Communicating Observations

Use your clinical training to provide clear, useful information:

  • Specific, Not General: "Reluctant to bear weight on right rear leg after rising from rest, improves after 5-10 minutes of movement" rather than "seems stiff"
  • Quantified When Possible: "Walks about 0.5 miles now compared to 2 miles six months ago"
  • Behavioral Context: "Panting at rest, primarily at night, averaging 3-4 episodes per week"
  • Video Documentation: Your clinical eye helps you capture the most diagnostic footage

Asking Informed Questions

Your knowledge allows you to dig deeper:

  • "What's your experience with multi-modal pain management for this condition?"
  • "Are there newer treatment options we should consider?"
  • "What would be your approach if cost weren't a factor? And what's the best option within my budget?"
  • "Can you explain the rationale for choosing this medication over alternatives?"
  • "What monitoring parameters should I watch for?"
  • "When should I escalate concerns versus manage at home?"

Discussing Treatment Plans

You can engage in more technical discussions:

  • Understanding drug interactions and contraindications
  • Discussing dose adjustments and timing
  • Asking about alternative administration routes if needed
  • Understanding the evidence base for recommendations
  • Discussing when to pursue diagnostics versus empirical treatment

When to Speak Up vs. When to Step Back

Speak Up When:

  • You notice something the veterinarian might have missed during the exam
  • The proposed plan doesn't align with current best practices you're aware of
  • You have concerns about drug interactions or contraindications
  • You need clarification on technical aspects
  • You're aware of resources or specialists they might not know about

Step Back When:

  • You're becoming emotional and can't think objectively
  • You're tempted to micromanage decisions
  • Your anxiety is making you question reasonable recommendations
  • You're projecting worst-case scenarios onto current situations
  • You need to trust your veterinary team to do their job

Navigating Difficult Dynamics

When You Disagree with Recommendations

Approach diplomatically:

  • "I've read about [alternative approach]. Can we discuss why you're recommending this instead?"
  • "I'm concerned about [specific issue]. Can you help me understand why this isn't a concern?"
  • "Would you be open to a second opinion or specialist consultation?"

If Your Knowledge Seems Threatening

Some veterinarians may feel defensive with knowledgeable clients:

  • Acknowledge their expertise: "I know my own limitations—I handle anesthesia/dental/etc., but this isn't my area"
  • Frame questions as learning: "Can you help me understand...?"
  • Show appreciation: "I really value having a veterinarian who's willing to discuss things at this level"

If defensiveness persists, this might not be the right fit for you. You need a veterinarian comfortable with your knowledge level.

Leveraging Your Professional Network

One significant advantage is access to colleagues:

Appropriate Use of Professional Network

  • General Advice: "Have you heard of any new treatments for DM?"
  • Referral Recommendations: "Can you recommend a pain management specialist?"
  • Second Opinions: From colleagues at other practices
  • Emotional Support: From colleagues who understand both the medical and emotional aspects

Inappropriate Use

  • Expecting free medical advice that replaces veterinary appointments
  • Asking colleagues to override your dog's primary veterinarian
  • Putting colleagues in uncomfortable positions regarding their employer or colleagues
  • Seeking diagnosis and treatment plans through Facebook messages

At-Home Care Skills

Your technical skills are a real advantage:

Skills You Can Apply

  • Subcutaneous fluid administration
  • Wound care and bandage changes
  • Precise medication administration
  • Vital sign monitoring
  • Blood glucose monitoring
  • Urinary catheterization if needed and authorized
  • Recognition of when to seek emergency care

Important Boundaries

  • Only perform procedures within your scope and comfort level
  • Don't replace veterinary care with home care
  • Recognize when emotional involvement impairs technical judgment
  • Follow prescribed protocols—don't improvise because you "know better"

The 24/7 On-Call Risk

Your skills can turn into a burden—you become your dog's 24/7 technician. Set boundaries. It's okay to say "I need help with this" even if you technically could do it yourself. Compassion fatigue is real, and self-care isn't selfish.

Managing the Emotional Toll

The Anticipatory Grief Burden

You likely start grieving earlier than lay pet owners because you know the trajectory:

  • You recognize early warning signs
  • You know what the eventual outcome likely looks like
  • You've seen similar cases at work
  • You know what "the end" involves

Coping Strategies

  • Compartmentalization (When Possible): Try to separate work experiences from your personal situation
  • Professional Support: Consider grief counseling, particularly from counselors familiar with veterinary professionals
  • Peer Support: Connect with other veterinary professionals who've been through this
  • Mindfulness: Focus on present quality of life rather than future decline
  • Permission to Not Be Strong: You don't have to be the stoic professional at home

The Euthanasia Decision

This is perhaps where your professional knowledge is both most helpful and most burdensome:

Your Advantage

  • Understanding of the process
  • Knowledge of quality of life assessment
  • Recognition of when suffering exists
  • Ability to advocate for peaceful process

Your Burden

  • Feeling you "should know" when it's time
  • Others expecting you to be more "rational"
  • Guilt about your knowledge—"I should have known sooner" or "Did I wait too long because I couldn't bear it?"
  • Having assisted with many euthanasias professionally but facing your own is entirely different

Remember

Being a veterinary professional doesn't make you less entitled to emotion, grief, or uncertainty. Your knowledge is a tool, but it doesn't eliminate the profound difficulty of losing your companion. Be as compassionate with yourself as you are with your clients.

Finding the Right Veterinary Partner

For RVTs, the veterinarian-client relationship has added dimensions:

Look For:

  • Comfort with your knowledge level
  • Willingness to engage in detailed discussions
  • Respect for your observations and skills
  • Recognition that you still need their expertise
  • Understanding of your unique emotional position

Red Flags:

  • Dismissing your concerns because you're "too close to see clearly"
  • Expecting you to do everything at home because "you can"
  • Being uncomfortable with your questions or knowledge
  • Making assumptions about your financial decisions

Your professional knowledge is a gift—it allows you to be an exceptional advocate for your senior dog. But it's also important to recognize when to let others carry some of the burden. You don't have to be the technician, the diagnostician, the emotional rock, and the grieving pet parent all at once. It's okay to just be the person who loves their dog and needs support through this difficult journey.

Action Steps

  • Evaluate whether your current veterinarian is the right partner for your knowledge level
  • Identify colleagues who can provide peer support
  • Set boundaries around your at-home care involvement
  • Consider professional counseling, particularly from those familiar with veterinary professionals
  • Give yourself permission to be emotional, uncertain, and "not the professional" at home
