The RVT Advantage

Using Your Professional Knowledge to Advocate Effectively During Appointments

← Back to Blog Series

Being a Registered Veterinary Technician (RVT) or other veterinary professional with your own senior dog is a unique and sometimes difficult position. You have the knowledge to recognize problems early, understand treatment options, and spot warning signs that others might miss. Yet you also face challenges that lay pet owners don't encounter. You're simultaneously the trained professional and the emotional pet parent. Let's explore how to leverage your knowledge while navigating this dual role.

The Double-Edged Sword of Knowledge

The Advantages

  • Early Recognition: You spot subtle changes that others miss
  • Informed Questions: You know what to ask and what information matters
  • Technical Understanding: You comprehend diagnoses, treatment rationales, and medication mechanisms
  • Skill Set: You can perform many at-home care tasks (SQ fluids, wound care, medication administration)
  • Professional Network: Access to colleagues for advice and referrals
  • Realistic Expectations: Understanding of what's achievable and what's not

The Challenges

  • Emotional Distance Impossible: Can't compartmentalize like you do at work
  • Knowledge of Worst-Case Scenarios: You've seen how bad things can get
  • Anticipatory Grief: Starts earlier because you know what's coming
  • Professional-Personal Boundary Blur: Difficulty separating your roles

Note: If you're struggling with compassion fatigue, see the NAVTA Wellness Resources for professional support.

Effective Advocacy During Appointments

Setting the Right Tone

You don't need scripts—you know how to talk to a vet. You just need permission to be assertive.

The approach: "I'm an RVT, so I'm familiar with medical terminology. Can we skip the client-speak and discuss this peer-to-peer?"

The "Peer-to-Peer" Conversation

Use technical language to cut through standard explanations and get to the real clinical discussion:

  • "I am concerned about central sensitization. Can we discuss an NMDA antagonist protocol?"
  • "I am tracking trendlines on appetite and mobility, not just today's status. Here's the data."
  • "Given the pain interference scores I'm seeing at home, should we revisit the analgesia ladder?"
  • "What are your thoughts on maropitant for the visceral component, in addition to what we're doing?"

If your veterinarian isn't receptive to this level of discussion, you may need a different partner for this journey.

When to Speak Up

  • You notice something the veterinarian might have missed during the exam
  • The proposed plan doesn't align with current guidelines (cite the AAHA/IAAHPC End-of-Life Care Guidelines if needed)
  • You have concerns about drug interactions or contraindications
  • You're aware of resources or specialists they might not know about

If your veterinarian is dismissive of your clinical knowledge, quote the guidelines, present your data, and request a referral if needed. You don't need to apologize for knowing things.

Your Professional Network

Use your professional connections appropriately:

  • Second Opinions: From colleagues at other practices or specialists
  • Referral Recommendations: "Can you recommend a pain management or rehab specialist?"
  • Research Access: If you have VIN access, use it. Pull the literature on your dog's condition.
  • Peer Support: Colleagues who understand both the medical and emotional weight of this

Weaponize Your Technical Skills

This is where you have a massive advantage over lay pet owners. Use it.

The Diagnostic Monitor (Weekly)

Skills you can apply that others cannot:

  • Sleeping Respiratory Rate (SRR): Track this weekly. You can catch congestive heart failure weeks before a cough starts. Normal is <30 breaths/min while sleeping. Trending upward? That's early warning.
  • Bladder Palpation: Check for urinary retention. A distended bladder that isn't emptying is a medical emergency owners miss.
  • Cardiac Auscultation: Listen for new murmurs, arrhythmias, or changes in heart sounds. You have the skills—use them.
  • Body Condition Score (BCS) & Muscle Condition Score (MCS): Track monthly. Muscle wasting is often missed until it's severe.
  • Weight: Weekly. Small changes matter.

The Treatment Nurse (What You Can Do At Home)

  • SQ Fluids: You can manage Stage 3/4 kidney disease at home for months longer than a layperson could. This is a huge quality of life advantage.
  • Injectable Medications: You can administer Adequan (polysulfated glycosaminoglycan) and B12 at home, saving hundreds of dollars and reducing clinic stress. Many RVTs also give Cerenia SQ for nausea.
  • Precise Medication Timing: You understand pharmacokinetics. You can optimize dosing schedules for peak effect.
  • Blood Glucose Monitoring: For diabetic dogs, home curves are less stressful and more accurate.
  • Wound Care: You can manage wounds, pressure sores, and bandage changes that would require vet visits for others.

The Case Manager

  • Organize the Medical File: Create a timeline of diagnoses, treatments, and responses. Your vet will thank you.
  • Graph the Trends: QoL scores, pain scores, SRR, weight—graph them. Trendlines tell the story that single data points can't.
  • Know the Drug Interactions: You can flag potential interactions before they become problems.
  • Track the Research: If you have VIN (Veterinary Information Network) access, pull the papers yourself. Don't wait for the vet to research your dog's condition.

The RVT Checklist

Your weekly monitoring protocol:

  • ☐ Sleeping Respiratory Rate recorded
  • ☐ Weight recorded
  • ☐ Body Condition Score assessed
  • ☐ Muscle Condition Score assessed
  • ☐ Quality of Life scale completed
  • ☐ Pain assessment completed
  • ☐ Trends graphed and reviewed
  • ☐ Medications inventoried (don't run out)

The Emotional Toll

You likely start grieving earlier than lay pet owners because you know the trajectory. You've seen similar cases at work. You know what "the end" involves. This is a burden, but it's also data—use it to make better decisions, not to torture yourself with anticipatory grief.

The Euthanasia Decision

Your professional knowledge is both your greatest asset and your greatest burden here:

  • Your Advantage: You understand the process. You recognize when suffering exists. You can advocate for a peaceful protocol.
  • Your Burden: You may feel you "should know" exactly when it's time. You may feel guilty for your knowledge—"I should have known sooner" or "Did I wait too long because I couldn't bear it?"

Permission Granted

Being a veterinary professional doesn't make you less entitled to emotion, grief, or uncertainty. Having assisted with hundreds of euthanasias professionally is entirely different from facing your own dog's death. Be as compassionate with yourself as you are with your clients.

Finding the Right Veterinary Partner

For RVTs, you need a veterinarian who is comfortable with your knowledge level, willing to engage in technical discussions, and respects that you still need their expertise—not their simplified client explanations.

Red Flags:

  • Dismissing your concerns because you're "too close to see clearly"
  • Expecting you to do everything at home because "you can"
  • Being uncomfortable with your questions or clinical language

Your professional knowledge is a gift. Use it to be an exceptional advocate. But also recognize when to let others carry some of the burden. You don't have to be the technician, the diagnostician, the case manager, and the grieving pet parent all at once.
