Building Your Senior Dog Care Team

Finding Veterinary Partners Who Understand Palliative Care

← Back to Blog Series

You wouldn't climb Mount Everest alone, and you shouldn't navigate senior dog care without a strong support team. The right veterinary partners can make the difference between simply managing decline and truly optimizing your senior dog's quality of life. But finding professionals who understand and embrace palliative care philosophy isn't always straightforward.

Understanding Palliative Care for Pets

Palliative care focuses on comfort, quality of life, and symptom management rather than cure. It's not the same as hospice care (which typically refers to end-of-life care in the final weeks or days). Palliative care can span months or even years, addressing chronic conditions while maintaining the best possible quality of life.

In veterinary medicine, palliative care means:

  • Prioritizing comfort and function over diagnostic pursuit
  • Managing pain and other symptoms proactively
  • Supporting both the patient and the family
  • Focusing on what the dog can still do and enjoy
  • Making decisions based on quality of life rather than quantity

Your Primary Veterinarian: The Foundation

Your primary care veterinarian is the cornerstone of your senior dog's care team. Ideally, this is someone who already knows your dog's history, personality, and baseline health. However, as your dog transitions into their senior years, you may need to reassess whether your current veterinary practice can meet evolving needs.

Questions to Ask Your Current Veterinarian

  • How do you approach chronic pain management in senior dogs?
  • What experience do you have with palliative care cases?
  • Are you comfortable with multi-modal pain management approaches?
  • How do you incorporate owner observations into treatment planning?
  • What's your philosophy on quality of life assessments?
  • Are you willing to try emerging therapies like ketamine for chronic pain?
  • Can you provide referrals to specialists when needed?

Green Flags: Signs of a Good Palliative Care Partner

  • Listens carefully to your observations about changes in behavior and comfort
  • Discusses multiple treatment options with pros and cons of each
  • Is willing to adjust treatments based on response rather than following a rigid protocol
  • Talks openly about quality of life and end-of-life planning without pressure
  • Respects your professional knowledge if you're an RVT or in veterinary field
  • Provides clear communication about what to watch for at home
  • Is accessible for questions between appointments

Red Flags: When to Consider Finding New Care

  • Dismisses your concerns with "they're just old"
  • Unwilling to try pain management because "we don't know if it's really painful"
  • Pushes for aggressive diagnostics without discussing palliative alternatives
  • Makes you feel rushed or judged during appointments
  • Lacks knowledge about current pain management options
  • Discourages second opinions or specialist consultation

Building Your Extended Team

Veterinary Specialists

Depending on your dog's specific needs, you may benefit from specialists:

  • Pain Management/Anesthesia Specialists: For complex chronic pain, ketamine therapy, or when standard medications aren't providing relief
  • Rehabilitation Veterinarians: For mobility issues, post-surgical recovery, or maintaining muscle mass and function
  • Internal Medicine Specialists: For managing complex chronic diseases like kidney disease, diabetes, or Cushing's disease
  • Veterinary Acupuncturists: For pain management, particularly in dogs who can't tolerate certain medications
  • Mobile Veterinarians: For dogs with severe mobility issues or anxiety at clinics

Allied Health Professionals

Don't overlook non-veterinary professionals who can support your senior dog:

  • Veterinary Physical Therapists/Rehabilitation Technicians: Can teach you exercises and techniques for home care
  • Certified Canine Massage Therapists: For comfort, circulation, and bonding
  • Professional Dog Trainers: Experienced with senior dogs can help with mobility assistance training and cognitive support

Making the Most of Veterinary Appointments

Once you've assembled your team, maximize the value of each appointment:

Before the Appointment

  • Keep a journal of symptoms, changes, good days vs. difficult days
  • Record videos of concerning behaviors (limping, trouble rising, etc.)
  • Write down your questions and concerns
  • List all current medications and supplements with dosages
  • Prepare to discuss your goals and priorities

During the Appointment

  • Start with your most important concerns in case time runs short
  • Share your observations about what makes your dog comfortable or uncomfortable
  • Ask questions when something isn't clear
  • Request written summaries or take notes
  • Discuss what to watch for before the next visit
  • Clarify who to contact with questions (primary vet, tech, specialist)

After the Appointment

  • Review the plan and make sure you understand all instructions
  • Set up any necessary follow-up appointments
  • Fill prescriptions promptly
  • Contact the clinic if you have questions while implementing the plan

When Geography or Finance Are Barriers

Not everyone has access to specialists or multiple veterinary options. If you're limited:

  • Telemedicine: Some specialists offer remote consultations to work with your local vet
  • Education: Bring research and resources to appointments to facilitate discussions about newer treatments
  • Second Opinions: Even if you can't regularly see a specialist, a one-time consultation can provide valuable guidance
  • Online Communities: Connect with other owners of senior dogs and veterinary professionals for support and information
  • Veterinary Schools: Teaching hospitals often have expertise in palliative care and may offer services at reduced cost

The Relationship Matters

The technical skills of your veterinary team are crucial, but so is the relationship. You need partners who:

  • Respect your knowledge of your dog
  • Support your decision-making without judgment
  • Acknowledge the emotional aspects of senior dog care
  • Work collaboratively rather than dictating care
  • Understand that you're advocating for quality over quantity

Building a strong senior dog care team takes effort, but it's one of the most important things you can do for your aging companion. With the right partners, you can navigate the challenges ahead with confidence, knowing you have expert support every step of the way.

Action Steps

  • Schedule a senior wellness consultation to discuss your veterinarian's approach to palliative care
  • Research specialists in your area who focus on pain management or rehabilitation
  • Join online communities or local groups for senior dog owners
  • Create a contact list with all team members' information and hours
  • Set up a system for tracking appointments, medications, and observations
