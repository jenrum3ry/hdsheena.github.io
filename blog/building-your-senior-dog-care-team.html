Understanding Palliative Care for Pets

Palliative care focuses on comfort, quality of life, and symptom management. Unlike hospice care (end-of-life care in final weeks/days), palliative care can span months or years.

Core principles:

  • Prioritizing comfort and function over diagnostic pursuit
  • Managing pain and other symptoms proactively
  • Supporting both the patient and the family
  • Focusing on what the dog can still do and enjoy
  • Making decisions based on quality of life rather than quantity

Your Primary Veterinarian: The Foundation

Your primary vet is the cornerstone of your team. As your dog ages, reassess whether your current practice can meet evolving needs.

Questions to Ask

  • How do you approach chronic pain management in senior dogs?
  • What experience do you have with palliative care?
  • Are you comfortable with multi-modal pain management?
  • How do you incorporate owner observations?
  • What's your philosophy on quality of life assessments?
  • Are you willing to try therapies like ketamine for chronic pain?
  • Can you provide specialist referrals when needed?

Green Flags

  • Listens to your observations about behavior changes
  • Discusses multiple treatment options
  • Adjusts treatments based on response
  • Talks openly about quality of life without pressure
  • Respects your knowledge of your dog
  • Provides clear home monitoring instructions
  • Accessible between appointments

Red Flags

  • Dismisses concerns with "they're just old"
  • Unwilling to try pain management
  • Pushes diagnostics without discussing palliative alternatives
  • Makes you feel rushed or judged
  • Lacks current pain management knowledge
  • Discourages second opinions

Building Your Extended Team

Veterinary Specialists

  • Pain Management/Anesthesia: Complex chronic pain, ketamine therapy, advanced pain control
  • Rehabilitation: Mobility issues, muscle maintenance, functional support
  • Internal Medicine: Kidney disease, diabetes, Cushing's disease
  • Acupuncture: Pain management when medications aren't tolerated
  • Mobile Vets: Severe mobility issues or clinic anxiety

Allied Health Professionals

  • Physical Therapists/Rehab Technicians: Home exercise programs
  • Canine Massage Therapists: Comfort and circulation support
  • Senior Dog Trainers: Mobility assistance training

Making the Most of Appointments

Before

  • Track symptoms, good vs. difficult days
  • Record videos (limping, trouble rising, pain behaviors)
  • List questions and current medications/supplements
  • Clarify your goals and priorities

During

  • Start with most important concerns
  • Share observations about comfort/discomfort patterns
  • Ask for clarification when needed
  • Take notes or request written summaries
  • Confirm who to contact with questions

After

  • Review plan and instructions
  • Schedule follow-ups
  • Fill prescriptions promptly
  • Contact clinic with questions

When Geography or Finance Are Barriers

  • Telemedicine: Remote specialist consultations with your local vet
  • Education: Bring research to facilitate treatment discussions
  • Second Opinions: One-time specialist consultation provides guidance
  • Online Communities: Connect with senior dog owners and professionals
  • Veterinary Schools: Teaching hospitals offer expertise at reduced cost

The Relationship Matters

Technical skills matter, but so does partnership. You need a team who respects your knowledge, supports your decisions, acknowledges emotional aspects, works collaboratively, and understands quality-over-quantity priorities.

Action Steps

  • Schedule senior wellness consultation to discuss palliative care approach
  • Research pain management or rehabilitation specialists
  • Join senior dog owner communities
  • Create team contact list
  • Set up tracking system for appointments, medications, observations
