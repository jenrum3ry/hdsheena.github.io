Quality of life assessments provide structure to objectively track decline, identify problems, and make data-driven decisions. They don't eliminate difficult choices, but they clarify them.

Why Assessments Matter

Common pitfalls without structure:

  • Adaptation bias - gradual acceptance of decline
  • Focus on brief good moments over difficult hours
  • Anticipatory grief clouding judgment
  • External pressure from others

Benefits of validated tools: Objective criteria, consistent tracking, improved veterinary communication, data-driven decisions.

Validated Quality of Life Tools

The Joy-to-Suffering Ratio

Core question: Is your dog experiencing more joy than suffering? When suffering (pain, confusion, distress) outweighs comfort and contentment, quality of life is unacceptable. All tools measure this ratio objectively.

OSU "How Do I Know When It's Time?" Scale

Ohio State University tool focusing on observable, measurable criteria rather than subjective interpretation.

Resource: OSU Assessment Tool (PDF)

HHHHHMM Quality of Life Scale (Dr. Alice Villalobos)

Most widely used tool. Evaluates seven criteria (0-10 each):

  • Hurt - Pain management
  • Hunger - Appetite and nutrition
  • Hydration - Fluid intake
  • Hygiene - Cleanliness, elimination control
  • Happiness - Joy expression, environmental response
  • Mobility - Movement ability
  • More good days than bad - Overall balance

Scoring: Above 35/70 = acceptable QoL. Below 35 = concerns need addressing.

JOURNEYS Quality of Life Scale

Interactive guided assessment with real-time scoring.

Resource: JOURNEYS Scale (PDF)

How to Use HHHHHMM

  • Complete the scale weekly or bi-weekly
  • Be honest—score based on reality, not what you wish were true
  • Track scores over time to identify trends
  • Use low-scoring categories to guide care adjustments
  • Share results with your veterinary team

Pain Assessment Tools

See Pain Recognition in Senior Dogs for CBPI, HCPI, and Glasgow Scale details. Use pain tools alongside QoL scales for comprehensive assessment.

MMVHS Quality of Life Assessment

Michigan State University scale. Evaluates physical health, mental well-being, overall quality. Numerical scoring tracks changes over time. Combine with pain tools (CBPI) for comprehensive data.

FETCH Quality of Life Checklist

  • Function and Mobility
  • Eating and Drinking
  • Tailored to Lifestyle (dog-specific activities)
  • Comfort and Pain Management
  • Hygiene and Self-Care

"Good Days vs. Bad Days" Tracking

Visual method creating objective data:

  • Mark daily: Good (green), Neutral (yellow), Bad (red)
  • Define categories based on dog's baseline
  • Review weekly for patterns
  • Bad days consistently > good days = time for conversation

Recommended: Hospet App

Hospet app - Industry standard for tracking. Visualizes trends, creates shareable data for veterinary team, removes emotional guesswork.

Define Your Criteria

  • Good: Stands independently, normal eating, outdoor activities, engages with family, comfortable resting
  • Neutral: Needs some help, reduced appetite but eating, less enthusiastic
  • Bad: Cannot stand/frequent falls, refuses food, won't go outside, pain behaviors, cannot get comfortable

Caregiver Burden: Your Quality of Life Matters

Caregiver fatigue is valid. Your quality of life affects your dog's care. You cannot provide adequate care from exhaustion.

Not Selfish

Destroying your health/relationships/career to extend life by weeks = crisis management, not sustainable care. Exhaustion doesn't make you a bad owner.

Caregiver burden factors:

  • Sleep deprivation - Nighttime anxiety/pacing/incontinence impairs judgment
  • Physical demands - Lifting, carrying, cleaning, medications
  • Financial stress - Mounting bills
  • Social isolation - Cannot leave house
  • Emotional exhaustion - Anticipatory grief + caregiving

Resource: Lap of Love - Quality of Life & Caregiver Assessment (PDF)

Critical Quality of Life Factors

Hygiene: Dignity

Be honest about incontinence. Dog soiling themselves/lying in it = dignity issue and QoL crisis.

Hygiene crisis signs:

  • Daily soiling of bedding
  • Lying in urine/feces
  • Skin breakdown, urine scalding, infections
  • Changing bedding multiple times daily
  • Pervasive odor in home
  • Considering confinement to hard surfaces

Nighttime Anxiety: Hidden Suffering

Sundowning is real. Pacing/panting/restlessness = mental anguish. Cognitive dysfunction often presents as nighttime anxiety. Dog may be confused, frightened, unable to settle.

Warning signs:

  • Pacing/wandering after dark
  • Panting at rest (not hot/pain)
  • Staring at walls/corners
  • Getting stuck in corners/behind furniture
  • Vocalizing without cause
  • Reversed sleep-wake cycle

Mental suffering counts. Perpetual confusion/fear ≠ good life.

Tracking Changes Over Time

Create a System

  • Consistency - Same day weekly/monthly
  • Multiple methods - Combine scores, calendar, journal
  • Video/Photo - Document walking, standing, body condition. Overcomes adaptation bias
  • Include family - Independent assessments, then compare

What to Track

  • Pain indicators (see Pain Recognition)
  • Mobility - distance, stairs, ease of rising
  • Appetite, water intake
  • Elimination - accidents, posture changes
  • Sleep quality - restlessness, duration
  • Social interaction, environmental interest
  • Response to favorite things

Recognizing Concerning Trends

Declining Trajectory

  • QoL scores trending downward over weeks/months
  • Good days less frequent or less good
  • Bad days more frequent or worse
  • Longer recovery from bad episodes
  • Interventions losing effectiveness

Warning Signs

  • Loss of interest in joy-producing activities
  • Uncomfortable more often than comfortable
  • Cannot do meaningful activities
  • More suffering than joy
  • Labored breathing at rest
  • Severe mobility loss - cannot stand/walk independently
  • Unmanaged pain despite all interventions

The "Is It Enough?" Question

Ask yourself regularly: "If my dog could tell me whether continuing like this is acceptable to them, what would they say?" This question cuts through our own desires and focuses on the dog's experience.

The Five Freedoms Framework

Minimum welfare baseline (not quality of life ceiling):

  1. Freedom from Hunger/Thirst
  2. Freedom from Discomfort
  3. Freedom from Pain/Injury/Disease
  4. Freedom to Express Normal Behavior
  5. Freedom from Fear/Distress

Note: These represent the floor, not the goal. Use as baseline check only. Joy-to-suffering ratio and validated tools are appropriate for evaluating quality of life.

Veterinary Input

Share assessments with veterinary team for:

  • Professional perspective on achievability
  • Additional intervention identification
  • Expectation calibration
  • Differentiation: suffering vs. manageable
  • Recognition of endpoint

When to Reassess Approach

If assessments show decline, ask: What else can we try?

  • Pain management optimization?
  • Unexplored treatments?
  • Specialist consultation?
  • Environmental modifications? (see Environmental Support)

Options exhausted + continued decline = time for end-of-life discussion (see End-of-Life Planning).

Avoiding Common Traps

Dogs Do Not "Tell You" It's Time

Dogs will not signal until catastrophic failure. Hardwired to hide weakness. They keep trying until physically unable. Waiting for a "sign" = waiting for ER crisis (collapse, seizure, respiratory distress).

Kindest decisions made before crisis. Use tools to recognize trajectory. Don't wait for the cliff.

Other Traps

  • "One more day" - Compassionate impulse can prolong suffering
  • "They're still eating" - Appetite is one factor; not sufficient alone
  • "Better a week early than a day late" - Preventing suffering > maximizing life moments

Support Resources

  • Friends who've experienced this
  • Anticipatory grief counseling
  • Family perspectives
  • Time-limited trials: "Optimize and reassess in 2 weeks"

Action Steps

  • Choose assessment tool, complete baseline today
  • Set up tracking (calendar/journal/spreadsheet/app)
  • Take baseline videos for comparison
  • Schedule regular assessments (weekly/bi-weekly)
  • Share system with veterinary team
  • Define QoL criteria with family
