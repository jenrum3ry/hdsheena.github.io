Quality of Life Assessments

Using Validated Tools, Tracking Changes, and Recognizing When Good Days Become Fewer

← Back to Blog Series

Perhaps the most difficult aspect of senior dog care is recognizing when we've crossed from extending quality life to simply extending life. We love our dogs fiercely, and that love can make objectivity challenging. Quality of life assessments provide structure to help us see clearly, track changes over time, and make decisions based on our dog's experience rather than our own emotions. They don't make the hard decisions easy, but they make them clearer.

Why Formal Quality of Life Assessments Matter

Without structure, we're prone to several pitfalls:

  • Adaptation Bias: We gradually adjust to changes, not noticing how much has been lost
  • Good Moment Focus: We cling to brief good moments while minimizing the difficult hours
  • Anticipatory Grief: Our own distress can cloud our assessment of the dog's experience
  • External Pressure: Well-meaning friends or family may influence our perception

Validated assessment tools help by:

  • Providing objective criteria to evaluate
  • Creating a consistent framework for comparison over time
  • Identifying areas of concern we might overlook
  • Facilitating clearer communication with veterinary teams
  • Supporting decision-making with data rather than just gut feeling

Validated Quality of Life Tools

The HHHHHMM Quality of Life Scale (Dr. Alice Villalobos)

Perhaps the most widely used tool, this scale evaluates seven criteria on a scale of 0-10:

  • Hurt: Pain management adequacy
  • Hunger: Adequate nutrition and appetite
  • Hydration: Adequate fluid intake
  • Hygiene: Ability to stay clean, manage elimination
  • Happiness: Does the dog express joy, respond to surroundings?
  • Mobility: Can the dog move adequately?
  • More good days than bad: Overall balance of quality

Scoring: A score above 35/70 suggests acceptable quality of life. Scores below 35 indicate quality of life concerns that need addressing.

How to Use HHHHHMM

  • Complete the scale weekly or bi-weekly
  • Be honest—score based on reality, not what you wish were true
  • Track scores over time to identify trends
  • Use low-scoring categories to guide care adjustments
  • Share results with your veterinary team

Pain Assessment Tools (CBPI, HCPI, Glasgow Scale)

For detailed information on pain-specific assessment tools including the Canine Brief Pain Inventory (CBPI), Helsinki Chronic Pain Index (HCPI), and Glasgow Composite Measure Pain Scale, see Article 3: Pain Recognition in Senior Dogs. These tools focus specifically on measuring pain intensity and interference, which is a critical component of overall quality of life assessment.

When evaluating quality of life, consider using pain assessment tools in combination with the broader quality of life scales described here to get a comprehensive picture of your dog's wellbeing.

MMVHS Quality of Life Assessment

The Michigan State University Modified Monash Veterinary Health Service Quality of Life scale provides another structured assessment option:

  • Multiple Domains: Evaluates physical health, mental well-being, and overall quality
  • Numerical Scoring: Provides objective data for tracking changes over time
  • Combined Use: Many owners use both pain assessment tools (like CBPI) and MMVHS weekly to get comprehensive quality of life data from multiple perspectives
  • Veterinary Communication: Sharing these standardized scores with your veterinary team helps ensure everyone is working from the same data

The FETCH Quality of Life Checklist

A more detailed assessment examining:

  • Function and Mobility
  • Eating and Drinking
  • Tailored to Your Lifestyle (what activities matter most to this dog)
  • Comfort and Pain Management
  • Hygiene and Self-Care

The "Good Days vs. Bad Days" Calendar

A simpler, visual tracking method:

  • Mark each day on a calendar: Good (green), Neutral (yellow), or Bad (red)
  • Define what constitutes each category based on your dog's baseline
  • Review monthly to see patterns
  • When bad days consistently outnumber good days, it's time for serious conversation

Defining Your Criteria

Establish specific, observable criteria for evaluating each day. For example:

  • Good Day: Able to stand independently without assistance, shows interest in food and eating normally, willing to go outside for bathroom breaks and short activities, engages with family members, comfortable resting positions
  • Neutral Day: Needs some assistance but can participate in basic activities, reduced appetite but still eating, less enthusiastic but not distressed
  • Bad Day: Cannot stand without significant help or falls frequently, refuses food or only eats with coaxing, reluctant or unable to go outside, withdrawn or showing pain behaviors, restless and unable to get comfortable

Having clear criteria removes ambiguity and makes tracking more objective.

The Five Freedoms Applied to Senior Dogs

The Five Freedoms, originally developed for livestock welfare, provide another framework:

  1. Freedom from Hunger and Thirst: Is your dog able and willing to eat and drink?
  2. Freedom from Discomfort: Can they rest comfortably? Do they have appropriate shelter and bedding?
  3. Freedom from Pain, Injury, or Disease: Is pain adequately managed? Are treatable conditions being addressed?
  4. Freedom to Express Normal Behavior: Can they engage in activities that are important to them?
  5. Freedom from Fear and Distress: Are they anxious, confused, or frightened?

When one or more freedoms can no longer be reasonably maintained, quality of life is significantly compromised.

Tracking Changes Over Time

Create a System

  • Consistency: Assess on the same day each week/month
  • Multiple Methods: Combine numerical scores with calendar tracking and journal notes
  • Video Documentation: Regular videos capture changes you might not otherwise notice; take short clips of your dog walking, standing up, or engaging in typical activities and date them for comparison with your veterinary team
  • Photo Documentation: Photographs can also document progression—body condition, posture, mobility aids in use, and overall appearance
  • Objective Assessment Value: Photos and videos provide objective documentation that helps overcome adaptation bias (where you gradually adjust to changes without noticing how much has been lost)
  • Include Family: Have other household members complete assessments independently, then compare

What to Track

  • Pain indicators (as discussed in earlier article)
  • Mobility (distance walked, stairs managed, ease of rising)
  • Appetite and eating behavior
  • Water intake
  • Elimination (any accidents, changes in posturing)
  • Sleep quality (restlessness, duration, positions)
  • Social interaction (with people, other pets)
  • Interest in environment (alert to sounds, watching activities)
  • Response to favorite things (treats, toys, people coming home)

Recognizing Concerning Trends

Declining Trajectory

Watch for these patterns:

  • Quality of life scores trending downward over weeks/months
  • Good days becoming less frequent or less good
  • Bad days becoming more frequent or worse
  • Recovery time from bad episodes increasing
  • Interventions that previously helped becoming less effective

Warning Signs

  • Loss of Interest: No longer responding to things that always brought joy
  • Persistent Discomfort: Appears uncomfortable more often than comfortable
  • Inability to Do Important Activities: Can't engage in what makes life meaningful for this individual dog
  • More Suffering Than Joy: The ratio has shifted unfavorably
  • Labored Breathing: Difficulty breathing even at rest
  • Severe Mobility Loss: Can't get up without help, can't walk without support
  • Unmanaged Pain: Despite all available interventions, pain persists

The "Is It Enough?" Question

Ask yourself regularly: "If my dog could tell me whether continuing like this is acceptable to them, what would they say?" This question cuts through our own desires and focuses on the dog's experience.

The Role of Veterinary Input

Share your quality of life assessments with your veterinary team:

  • They can provide professional perspective on what's achievable
  • They can identify if there are additional interventions to try
  • They can help calibrate your expectations
  • They can tell you if what you're seeing is suffering or manageable
  • They can support you in recognizing when you've done everything possible

When to Reassess Your Approach

If quality of life assessments show decline, first ask: Is there something else we can try?

  • Have we optimized pain management?
  • Are there treatments we haven't explored?
  • Is specialist consultation appropriate?
  • Have we addressed environmental factors?
  • Is there anything improving comfort that we're not doing?

If you've exhausted reasonable options and quality continues to decline, assessments help guide the difficult conversation about end-of-life timing.

The Subjective Component

While tools provide structure, they don't eliminate the subjective element:

  • You Know Your Dog: You understand what's important to them
  • Individual Values: Some families prioritize longevity; others prioritize absence of suffering
  • Cultural and Personal Factors: These legitimately influence decisions
  • Financial Realities: Available resources do affect choices

The goal isn't to make the "perfect" decision—there rarely is one. The goal is to make an informed, compassionate decision based on honest assessment of your dog's quality of life.

Avoiding Common Traps

"Wait for a Sign"

Many owners wait for their dog to "tell them when it's time." The reality: dogs can't tell us. They'll keep trying until they can't. Waiting for absolute certainty often means waiting too long.

"One More Day"

"One more good day" is a compassionate impulse, but can lead to prolonging suffering. Regular assessments help identify when "one more day" has turned into too many difficult days.

"They're Still Eating"

Appetite is just one factor. Dogs can eat and still have poor quality of life in other domains.

"Better a Week Too Early Than a Day Too Late"

This phrase resonates with many. It reflects the value of preventing suffering over maximizing every possible moment of life.

Supporting Your Assessment Process

  • Talk to Others: Friends who have gone through this, support groups, online communities
  • Counseling: Anticipatory grief counseling can help you process emotions without clouding judgment
  • Multiple Perspectives: Family members may notice things you don't
  • Time-Limited Trials: "Let's optimize everything possible and reassess in two weeks"

Quality of life assessments are tools for love, not judgment. They help us honor our commitment to our dogs by ensuring we're making decisions in their best interest, not just our own. The goal isn't to find the perfect moment—it's to recognize when continuing means more suffering than joy, and to act with courage and compassion when that time comes.

Action Steps

  • Choose a quality of life assessment tool and complete it today as a baseline
  • Set up a tracking system (calendar, journal, spreadsheet)
  • Take videos of your dog today to compare with future videos
  • Schedule regular assessment times (weekly or bi-weekly)
  • Share your assessment system with your veterinary team
  • Discuss with family members what quality of life means for your dog
