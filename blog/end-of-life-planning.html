End-of-life planning = logistics. Handle paperwork in advance so you can be present with your dog instead of scrambling.

Goal: Project Manager of Death

Most loving action: handle logistics in advance = just be a pet owner when it matters. This is about protocols and decisions, not feelings.

Resource: OVC Pet Trust (U of Guelph) - Clinical gold standard.

Why Plan Ahead

Crisis decisions = harder. Emotions overwhelming, 2 AM choices, no regular vet available, prolonged suffering during logistics, family disagreements, financial obstacles.

Plan ahead = focus on dog, not paperwork.

Recognizing the Transition

Chronic senior care ≠ end-of-life. See Building Your Care Team for palliative vs. hospice care distinction.

Palliative Care Phase (months-years)

  • Acceptable QoL with management
  • Good days ≥ difficult days
  • Engages in meaningful activities
  • Treatments provide benefit

Hospice/End-of-Life Phase (days-weeks)

  • Rapid decline despite care
  • Difficult days > good days
  • Loss of interest in activities
  • Multiple system failure
  • Treatments minimally effective
  • QoL clearly compromised

Explicit Transition Decision

Curative → Comfort-Focused: When function declines despite optimal interventions (e.g., dragging feet despite aids, pain vs. neurological decline indistinguishable), explicitly shift to comfort-only focus.

Document: "November 2025: shifted to comfort-focused care" - clarifies goals going forward.

Making the Decision

Use QoL assessment tools including "Good vs. Bad Days" tracking.

No Perfect Time

Rarely clear moment. Best decision with imperfect info + overwhelming emotions. Permission granted: compassionate decision ≠ "perfect" decision.

Guiding Questions

  • More suffering than joy?
  • Can do meaningful activities?
  • Dignity maintained?
  • Prolonging life or death?
  • For them or me?
  • What would they choose?
  • Regret waiting > acting now?
  • Pain vs. neuro decline distinguishable/treatable?

Better a Week Too Early Than a Day Too Late

Prevent suffering > maximize moments. Never ready, but dogs deserve peace before unbearable suffering.

Code Status & Advanced Directives

Emergency "Code Status"

Decide before 2 AM ER:

  • DNR: CPR in geriatric dogs = poor outcomes, significant trauma. Know answer in advance
  • Emergency Surgery: Under what circumstances? Bloat? Splenic mass? Age/stage limits?
  • Financial Cap: "Limit = $X. Above that, euthanize." Not selfish. Realistic
  • QoL Thresholds: Unacceptable outcomes even if survival possible? ("Never walk again = no treatment")

Write It Down

Document. Share with vet (file copy), emergency contacts, pet sitters. In crisis, Past You makes hard calls for Future You.

Euthanasia Logistics (document now)

  • Location - Clinic, home (mobile vet), outdoors (favorite spot)?
  • Present - Family, friends, other pets?
  • Aftercare - Cremation (private/communal), aquamation, burial?
  • Mementos - Paw prints, fur, nose print?

Pay In Advance

Don't fumble for credit card while dog is dead. Call clinic 24h prior, pay by phone. Sign consent digitally or arrive early for paperwork.

Not cold = logistics allowing presence instead of reception desk crying.

Share Plan

  • Family consensus
  • Vet (file copy)
  • Emergency contacts/pet sitters
  • Update as needed

Euthanasia Options

In-Clinic

+ Equipment available, staff support, less expensive, familiar to team
- Travel stress, clinical environment, encounter others, drive home grieving

At-Home

+ Familiar environment, no travel stress, privacy, meaningful location, other pets can process
- More expensive, not all vets offer, may need body transport, memory in home

Two-Injection Protocol

1. IM Sedation ("Goodnight")

  • Muscle injection (usually hind leg)
  • Sedative + pain med combination
  • Dog deeply relaxed/sleepy over 10-15min
  • Unconscious before #2
  • Can hold/pet/talk during

2. IV Euthanasia (Final)

  • Barbiturate overdose (pentobarbital)
  • IV (usually front leg)
  • Immediate unconsciousness (already unconscious from sedation)
  • Breathing stops (seconds), heart stops (shortly after)

Normal Body Responses (Not Distress)

  • Agonal breathing - 1-3 gasps after heart stops. Brainstem reflex, not conscious
  • Bladder/bowels - Muscle relaxation. Bring towel
  • Eyes open - May drift open after closing
  • Muscle twitches - Nerve firing, not conscious

Nothing went wrong = biology.

Day Before: Checklist

  • ☐ Confirm appointment (time, location, who)
  • ☐ Pay invoice by phone - Not at checkout
  • ☐ Sign consent digitally or arrive early
  • ☐ Confirm aftercare
  • ☐ Arrange transport home (don't drive yourself)
  • ☐ Tissues, blankets, dog's bed ready
  • ☐ Notify who should be present
  • ☐ Clear calendar (non-functional after)

If condition allows: favorite activities/foods (no diet restrictions), gentle time, photos. Don't force "perfect day" if suffering. Kindest gift sometimes = end suffering sooner.

Body Care: Cremation vs. Aquamation

Flame Cremation

1400-1800°F combustion, 1-3hrs, gritty ash with bone fragments, higher carbon, widely available, less expensive

Aquamation (Alkaline Hydrolysis)

Water-based alkaline solution + gentle heat (200-300°F), 6-20hrs, fine white powder (20% more remains), 1/10th carbon footprint, growing in Canada, similar cost

Resource: Gateway Pet Memorial (Canada)

Burial & Memorials

  • Home burial - Check regulations, consider permanence
  • Pet cemetery - Maintained site
  • Keepsakes - Paw/nose prints, fur, urns, jewelry, memorial trees/stones

Grief Resources

  • ASPCA Pet Loss Hotline, Lap of Love support, local groups
  • Pet loss counselors, grief therapists
  • Crisis: 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)
  • APLB, IAAHPC

Other pets may search/show behavior changes. Some benefit from presence during/after (helps understanding). Monitor prolonged changes.

You Did Everything

Every accommodation, assessment, documented decision = acts of love. Final act: releasing from suffering when you'd give anything to keep them. Not giving up = showing up.
